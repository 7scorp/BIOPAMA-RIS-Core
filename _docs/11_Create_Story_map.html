How to develop your story

Use the guidelines below to develop your story based on the data and information collected.

Reflect on the following points before developing your story:

Tips

  • Target audience: is it scientific, political, or technical ?
  • Terminology: is your target audience familiar with the terms you are using?
  • Purpose: what is the ultimate message behind this story?
  • The 'So what?': how your data and information can lead (or have led) to better decisions?

'The 4 Cs'

  • Contents: do you have the right to use all maps, legends, data, photos, information, etc.?
  • Credits: be sure that copyright for the above elements are addressed appropriately.
  • Consistency: use the same format all along the story (e.g. always include map legends in the same space).
  • Congruence: use paragraphs and sub-paragraphs to talk about topics that are connected. (e.g. SPECIES - Elephants, Rhinos, etc.).

Structure

  • Introduction:
  • Key messages: risks and benefits of, e.g. monitoring a specific issue or engaging in a specific conservation action.
  • Conclusion: solution, best practice, achievement, or biodiversity target achieved.

Story map elements and examples

Element Contents Example
Cover Title and BIOPAMA logo What future for People in Tanzania?
Introduction Introduce the issue. Describe why important. Describe the map. A human-dominated Planet. Human pressure can be observed on the map.
Section break A sentence can introduce the story. This story is about human pressure on land in Tanzania.
Descriptive section Narrow down the attention on your topic. Introduce the country/protected area(s). Provide general information. Tanzania one of the largest countries of East Africa. A large share of the population relies on subsistence farming for living.
Map section Describe why you collected these data, the source, the difficulties encountered. What are you displaying on the map? What are the implications? What each color represents?. The map shows an index of biodiversity richness (how many species live in the same site) and rarity (how much common are those species).
Data visualisation section Include graphs (embedded from external sources or created as a jpeg). The graph shows biodiversity richness (how many species live in the same site) and rarity (how much common are those species).
